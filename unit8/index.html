About Me

Hello! I'm Charlotte, a 22-year-old Connecticut native who much prefers Vermont. In the past 12 months, I graduated Summa cum laude from Middlebury College, moved to Washington, D.C., and landed a job at a SaaS company called Opower. While pursuing my B.A., I studied Environmental Economics, spent a language-intensive semester abroad in Madrid, Spain, and tutored peers in writing and editing. In the summers, I pursued my passion for langauge through working in journalism and communications. As a senior, I carried out independent research on the potential for offshore wind energy development on the the Atlantic Coast, which sparked my professional interest in the energy sector. The opportunity for growth, innovation, and leadership in the clean technology and energy efficiency spaces is what motivates me today.

In My Own Words.
What's an Environmental Economics degree? It's an interdiscplinary major that involves coursework in policy, literature, economics, and the natural sciences. Think: Silent Spring, Pigouvian taxes, NEPA, ecosystem services, Love Canal, the whooping crane, Kyoto Protocol, etc.


My courses took me to the New Haven and Middlebury Rivers to study insect communities, local farms to evaluate the impact of Tropical Storm Irene on local agriculture, and the capitol building in Montpelier to discuss the demand for a state-wide carbon tax. My experience across the disciplines has positioned me to tackle complex environmental issues with a unique understanding of the myriad of factors involved. Additionally, I worked closely with several computer programs, including STATA, IDRISI, ArcGIS and Quantum GIS.


While not in class, I enjoyed the Vermont outdoors, namely through skiing, hiking, and running. I also worked as a a Peer Writing Tutor for the College's Center for Teaching Learning and Research (CTLR). Each semester, I was assigned an introductory writing course of over 20 students. I met with them weekly and became their mentor for writing and editing, as well as time management skills.
